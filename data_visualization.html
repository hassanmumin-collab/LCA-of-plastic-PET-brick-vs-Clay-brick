Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): Clay Brick vs. PET Plastic Brick

This report presents a comparative Life Cycle Assessment of traditional clay bricks against innovative PET plastic bricks, with a functional unit of 1 brick.

Executive Summary & Strategic Recommendation

Overview

This analysis reveals a stark contrast in the environmental performance of PET bricks versus traditional clay bricks. The PET brick demonstrates significant environmental benefits, primarily driven by the "avoided burden" of plastic waste recycling. In contrast, the high-energy firing process for clay bricks results in substantial environmental impacts, particularly in energy consumption and climate change contributions.

Methodology

Functional Unit: This assessment uses 1 brick as the functional unit. This standardized basis allows for direct and fair comparison of environmental impacts between clay bricks and PET plastic bricks on a per-unit basis.

Brick Specifications:

  • Clay Brick: 3.5 kg per brick (dimensions 19×19×13.5 cm). Standard masonry unit used in East African construction with typical void ratio.
  • PET Plastic Brick: 1.8 kg per brick (approximately 49% lighter than clay brick). The significantly lower weight reflects the density difference between plastic materials (1.2–1.4 g/cm³) and fired clay (1.8+ g/cm³), while maintaining similar functional dimensions for interlocking lego-type assembly.

Life Cycle Assessment Approach:

  • Clay Brick Boundary: "Cradle-to-Use" - encompasses raw material extraction (clay mining), material processing, manufacturing (high-temperature kiln firing), transportation to site, packaging, and the use phase including mortar (cement) application during construction.
  • PET Brick Boundary: "Cradle-to-Gate with Avoided Burden" - begins with waste plastic collection from post-consumer sources, material sorting and processing, manufacturing into bricks, transportation, and packaging. Critically, this assessment includes the avoided burden principle, giving environmental credits for diverting waste from landfilling.

Impact Categories Assessed:

  • Climate Change (kg CO2e/brick): Measures global warming potential from greenhouse gas emissions throughout the lifecycle.
  • Energy Demand (MJ/brick): Quantifies cumulative non-renewable energy consumption.
  • Water Depletion (L/brick): Indicates freshwater resource consumption.

Key Methodological Feature - Avoided Burden: The PET brick's environmental profile includes credits for avoiding the negative impacts that would result from landfilling the waste plastic. This reflects the circular economy principle that recycling waste materials prevents future environmental damage, making the comparison a true "avoided burden" assessment rather than a simple substitution scenario.

⚠️ Important Limitations and Assumptions:

  • End-of-Life Stage Not Included: This assessment covers Cradle-to-Gate (with use phase for clay bricks) but does NOT model end-of-life disposal. Future recycling, reuse, or decomposition of either brick type is not included in results.
  • PET Avoided Burden Assumptions: The large negative values for PET (-5.34 kg CO2e/brick climate, -3.34 g PM2.5/brick) assume that 100% of collected waste plastic would otherwise be landfilled with methane capture. Alternative end-of-life scenarios (open burning, energy recovery, additional recycling) would significantly change these credits—potentially by ±30-50%.
  • Mortar Calculation: Clay brick use phase assumes 0.15 kg cement mortar per kg of brick (10-12mm joint thickness, standard masonry). Regional construction practices may vary: 0.08-0.20 kg mortar/kg brick depending on joint thickness and workmanship.
  • Grid Electricity Carbon Intensity: All values assume Kenya/East African electricity grid mix (~0.4 kg CO2e/kWh based on ~30% hydro, ~30% thermal, ~40% other renewables). Actual carbon intensity varies by region and time of year.
  • Regional Adaptation: Data sourced from peer-reviewed LCA studies of fired clay bricks (Greece, Thailand, Pakistan) and PET recycling (global). Adaptation to Kenya/East African context applies published emission factors but assumes similar technology and efficiency levels.
  • Uncertainty Ranges: Conservative estimates used throughout. Climate change values may vary ±20-30% depending on specific kiln technology (clay) or collection efficiency (PET).

Key Findings - Detailed Results per Brick

  • Climate Change: PET brick delivers a net climate benefit of 1.35 kg CO₂e saved per brick (197% net benefit). Clay brick emits +0.69 kg CO₂e/brick (raw material 0.01 + manufacturing 0.49 + transport 0.004 + mortar 0.18 + EOL 0.0002). PET brick emits -0.66 kg CO₂e/brick (raw material 0.81 + manufacturing 0.50 - avoided burning 1.98 + transport 0.002 + EOL 0). The dramatic benefit comes from diverting 1.8 kg of waste plastic from open burning, offsetting all upstream emissions.
  • Energy Demand: Clay brick is significantly more energy-efficient at 7.92 MJ per brick (71% better) versus 27.03 MJ for PET. Clay breaks down as: raw material 0.09 + manufacturing 7.0 + transport 0.06 + mortar 0.77 MJ. PET breaks down as: raw material 23.4 + manufacturing 3.6 + transport 0.03 MJ. PET recycling (13 MJ/kg) is highly energy-intensive; even the lighter PET brick requires substantially more energy than clay manufacturing.
  • Water Depletion: PET brick uses significantly less water at 4.50 L per brick (37% better) versus 7.18 L for clay. Clay water consumption is dominated by the manufacturing stage (2.02 L/kg × 3.5 kg = 7.07 L for clay preparation and firing) plus mortar production (0.032 L/kg × 3.5 kg = 0.112 L). PET water comes entirely from recycling facility washing and PET resin processing (2.5 L/kg × 1.8 kg = 4.5 L). In water-stressed regions, this is a significant advantage for PET bricks.
  • System Boundary Trade-offs: PET wins decisively on climate (saves 1.35 kg CO₂e per brick) and water consumption (uses 37% less). However, clay brick is significantly more energy-efficient, using 71% less energy per brick (7.92 vs 27.03 MJ). The trade-off is clear: PET excels in climate and water; clay excels in energy efficiency. The choice depends on regional priorities (climate vulnerability vs. water scarcity vs. energy availability).

End of lifeClimate change (reuse)0-The best climate scenario foreseen for PET brick end of life is reuse — demolished lego-type interlocking bricks are disassembled and directly reused in another structure without further processing. This is made possible by the no-mortar lego design, which allows manual separation without mechanical demolition equipment. Under this pathway EoL GHG emissions are effectively zero — no diesel collection vehicles, no transport to landfill, no processing energy, and no combustion. Two conditions must hold for this scenario to be achievable: first, the bricks must retain sufficient structural integrity after their service life; second, PET is susceptible to UV degradation over long periods of outdoor exposure, which may reduce compressive strength over a 20–30 year service life. No published data on long-term structural performance of PET lego bricks under East African climate conditions was found — this is a material data gap that should be addressed before scaling this system.

Note on Packaging Stage: The packaging life cycle stage represents the minimal environmental impact associated with bundling and preparing finished bricks for shipment. This includes plastic strapping material and the energy required for packaging production. The impacts are negligible because bricks require only simple protective wrapping (plastic straps) rather than heavy or complex packaging materials. This contrasts sharply with the manufacturing stage, which dominates the overall lifecycle impacts due to kiln firing (clay) or molding energy (PET).

Calculation Notes

Negative Impact Values (Avoided Burden): The negative value for PET brick's Climate Change impact reflects the "avoided burden" principle. By recycling PET plastic waste that would otherwise be open-burned (the dominant waste disposal method in Kenya), the brick is credited with avoiding methane and other GHG emissions from that burning. The 1.98 kg CO₂e credit for avoided burning more than offsets all upstream emissions (raw material 0.81 + manufacturing 0.50 + transport 0.002 kg CO₂e), yielding a net negative (beneficial) climate impact of -0.66 kg CO₂e per brick.

  • Climate Change: The credit for avoiding potent greenhouse gas emissions from open burning outweighs the emissions from the brick's own manufacturing and transportation. System expansion method per ISO 14044.
  • Particulate Matter: PET production generates no PM₂.₅ according to available data, while clay kiln firing is a significant particulate source (7.53 g PM₂.₅/brick, primarily from kiln combustion).
  • Energy Trade-off: PET requires substantially more energy (27.03 MJ) than clay (7.92 MJ) because recycled PET processing (13 MJ/kg) is highly energy-intensive. This is a critical trade-off: PET wins on climate and air quality, but clay wins decisively on energy and water consumption.

Calculation Method: The total impact for each category is the sum of all values from each life cycle stage detailed in the "Data Inventory" tab. This provides a comprehensive "Cradle-to-Use" (for Clay) and "Cradle-to-Gate with Avoided Burden" (for PET) assessment per brick.
