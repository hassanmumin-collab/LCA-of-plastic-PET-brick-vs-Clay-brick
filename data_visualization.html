Life Cycle Assessment (LCA): Clay Brick vs. PET Plastic Brick

This report presents a comparative Life Cycle Assessment of traditional clay bricks against innovative PET plastic bricks, with a functional unit of 1 brick.

Executive Summary & Strategic Recommendation

Overview

This analysis reveals a stark contrast in the environmental performance of PET bricks versus traditional clay bricks. The PET brick demonstrates significant environmental benefits, primarily driven by the "avoided burden" of plastic waste recycling. In contrast, the high-energy firing process for clay bricks results in substantial environmental impacts, particularly in energy consumption and climate change contributions.

Methodology

Functional Unit: This assessment uses 1 brick as the functional unit. This standardized basis allows for direct and fair comparison of environmental impacts between clay bricks and PET plastic bricks on a per-unit basis.

Brick Specifications:

  • Clay Brick: 2.8 kg per brick (dimensions 19×19×13.5 cm). Source: Yüksek & Öztaş (2020) study of fired clay brick production in Turkey, representing standard masonry units used in East African construction.
  • PET Plastic Brick: 2.24 kg per brick (approximately 20% lighter than clay brick). The lighter weight reflects the density difference between plastic materials (1.2–1.4 g/cm³) and fired clay (1.8+ g/cm³), while maintaining similar functional dimensions.

Life Cycle Assessment Approach:

  • Clay Brick Boundary: "Cradle-to-Use" - encompasses raw material extraction (clay mining), material processing, manufacturing (high-temperature kiln firing), transportation to site, packaging, and the use phase including mortar (cement) application during construction.
  • PET Brick Boundary: "Cradle-to-Gate with Avoided Burden" - begins with waste plastic collection from post-consumer sources, material sorting and processing, manufacturing into bricks, transportation, and packaging. Critically, this assessment includes the avoided burden principle, giving environmental credits for diverting waste from landfilling.

Impact Categories Assessed:

  • Climate Change (kg CO2e/brick): Measures global warming potential from greenhouse gas emissions throughout the lifecycle.
  • Energy Demand (MJ/brick): Quantifies cumulative non-renewable energy consumption.
  • Water Depletion (L/brick): Indicates freshwater resource consumption.
  • Particulate Matter (g PM2.5/brick): Measures emissions of fine particulate matter harmful to human health and ecosystems.
  • Land Use (m²a/brick): Represents the area of land occupied or transformed per unit of product.

Key Methodological Feature - Avoided Burden: The PET brick's environmental profile includes credits for avoiding the negative impacts that would result from landfilling the waste plastic. This reflects the circular economy principle that recycling waste materials prevents future environmental damage, making the comparison a true "avoided burden" assessment rather than a simple substitution scenario.

⚠️ Important Limitations and Assumptions:

  • End-of-Life Stage Not Included: This assessment covers Cradle-to-Gate (with use phase for clay bricks) but does NOT model end-of-life disposal. Future recycling, reuse, or decomposition of either brick type is not included in results.
  • PET Avoided Burden Assumptions: The large negative values for PET (-5.34 kg CO2e/brick climate, -3.34 g PM2.5/brick) assume that 100% of collected waste plastic would otherwise be landfilled with methane capture. Alternative end-of-life scenarios (open burning, energy recovery, additional recycling) would significantly change these credits—potentially by ±30-50%.
  • Mortar Calculation: Clay brick use phase assumes 0.15 kg cement mortar per kg of brick (10-12mm joint thickness, standard masonry). Regional construction practices may vary: 0.08-0.20 kg mortar/kg brick depending on joint thickness and workmanship.
  • Grid Electricity Carbon Intensity: All values assume Kenya/East African electricity grid mix (~0.4 kg CO2e/kWh based on ~30% hydro, ~30% thermal, ~40% other renewables). Actual carbon intensity varies by region and time of year.
  • Regional Adaptation: Data sourced from peer-reviewed LCA studies of fired clay bricks (Greece, Thailand, Pakistan) and PET recycling (global). Adaptation to Kenya/East African context applies published emission factors but assumes similar technology and efficiency levels.
  • Uncertainty Ranges: Conservative estimates used throughout. Climate change and particulate matter values may vary ±20-30% depending on specific kiln technology (clay) or collection efficiency (PET).

Key Findings

  • Climate Change: The PET brick delivers a substantial net climate benefit of 6.73 kg CO2e saved per brick compared to clay. The PET brick achieves -5.34 kg CO2e/brick (avoided burden from landfill diversion), while the clay brick emits 1.39 kg CO2e/brick. This dramatic difference stems from the avoided methane emissions that would result from landfilling waste plastic.
  • Energy Demand: The PET brick requires 3.96 MJ per brick versus 14.21 MJ for clay—a 72% energy reduction. Clay brick manufacturing is dominated by high-temperature kiln firing, which is inherently energy-intensive. PET brick processing uses electrical molding at lower temperatures, resulting in substantially lower cumulative energy demand.
  • Particulate Matter: The PET brick avoids 5.67 g PM2.5 per brick compared to clay. PET achieves -3.34 g PM2.5/brick (avoided from landfill decomposition), while clay emits 2.33 g PM2.5/brick. This is particularly significant for air quality and public health, especially in regions with existing air pollution challenges.
  • Water & Land Use: The PET brick reduces water consumption by 74% (1.12 L vs. 4.28 L per brick), primarily because clay brick manufacturing requires extensive water for clay processing and mortar production. Land use similarly favors PET, with the brick providing a net benefit of -0.0034 m²a/brick (avoided land impact from landfill diversion) versus clay's 0.005 m²a/brick.

Note on Packaging Stage: The packaging life cycle stage represents the minimal environmental impact associated with bundling and preparing finished bricks for shipment. This includes plastic strapping material and the energy required for packaging production. The impacts are negligible because bricks require only simple protective wrapping (plastic straps) rather than heavy or complex packaging materials. This contrasts sharply with the manufacturing stage, which dominates the overall lifecycle impacts due to kiln firing (clay) or molding energy (PET).

Calculation Notes

Negative Impact Values (Avoided Burden): The significant negative values for the PET brick's Climate Change, Particulate Matter, and Land Use impacts are due to the "avoided burden" principle. By recycling PET plastic waste that would otherwise end up in a landfill or uncontrolled dump, the brick is credited with avoiding the environmental impacts that waste would have caused. For example:

  • Climate Change: The credit for avoiding potent greenhouse gas emissions (like methane from landfill decomposition) outweighs the emissions from the brick's own manufacturing and transportation.
  • Particulate Matter & Land Use: Similarly, the brick is credited for preventing pollution and land use associated with waste disposal sites.

Calculation Method: The total impact for each category is the sum of all values from each life cycle stage detailed in the "Data Inventory" tab. This provides a comprehensive "Cradle-to-Use" (for Clay) and "Cradle-to-Gate with Avoided Burden" (for PET) assessment per brick.
